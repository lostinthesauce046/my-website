Oriental Orthodoxy

Overview

Oriental Orthodoxy is a branch of Eastern Christianity that includes several autocephalous churches. It is distinct from Eastern Orthodoxy and is characterized by its adherence to the teachings of the early ecumenical councils, particularly the Council of Chalcedon in 451 AD, which they rejected.

History

The Oriental Orthodox Churches trace their origins to the early Christian communities in the Middle East, North Africa, and the Indian subcontinent. The schism that led to the formation of Oriental Orthodoxy occurred after the Council of Chalcedon, which resulted in the separation of the non-Chalcedonian churches from those that accepted the council's definitions of Christology.

Beliefs

Oriental Orthodox Christians believe in the one nature of Christ (Miaphysitism), which asserts that in the person of Jesus Christ, divine and human natures are united in one nature without separation, confusion, or alteration. This belief contrasts with the Chalcedonian definition, which states that Christ exists in two natures, divine and human.

Churches

The main churches that comprise Oriental Orthodoxy include:

  • Coptic Orthodox Church: Predominantly found in Egypt, it is one of the oldest Christian communities in the world.
  • Armenian Apostolic Church: The national church of Armenia, known for its rich liturgical traditions.
  • Syriac Orthodox Church: Based in the Middle East, it has a significant presence in Syria and India.
  • Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church: The largest Christian denomination in Ethiopia, known for its unique traditions and practices.
  • Eritrean Orthodox Tewahedo Church: The national church of Eritrea, closely related to the Ethiopian Orthodox Church.

Practices

Oriental Orthodox Churches have rich liturgical traditions, including the use of ancient languages in their services, such as Coptic, Armenian, and Ge'ez. They celebrate the sacraments, including baptism, confirmation, and the Eucharist, with a strong emphasis on community and family life.
